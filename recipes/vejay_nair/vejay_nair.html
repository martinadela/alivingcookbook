Memory & Achar

Vejay Nair

Wandering Fermented Chutney

Fitzgerald and Petrick argue that, “we depend on our previous gastronomic experiences to try to taste the past1”. Every culinary encounter is tucked away in the vaults of our mind. Over time as our palates grow and change, we retain tastes of the past. Each of our “memory palates” is uniquely developed over time. No two individuals can share the same palate, culinary experience, or sense of taste. Nor can one hope to duplicate another person’s sense of taste.

I want to offer you a glimpse into my “memory palate”, through two different recipes. The first recipe has traveled from Punjab to the United States and then back across the Atlantic to Finland. My Nani’s2 Gobhi Shalgam Achar3. Achar is a condiment found in almost every South Asian home. Commonly made with spicy pickled fruits, vegetables, chilies or even seafood. Deeply acidic, subtly sweet, heavily spiced and uniquely pungent. It offers an added punch to any meal.

The second recipe is a Wandering Fermented Chutney, inspired by the achar’s and our family’s movement. As Ghassan Hage states, via Émile Benventiste, food is a core component of “the building of the feeling of being at home”4. This fermented chili chutney takes inspiration from the different stops on my Nani’s recipe’s journey. Adjusting to local tastes and available flavors as the nature of home shifts. While still allowing one to indulge in taste-based reminiscence.

Gobhi Shalgam Achar

3 kg vegetables (cauliflower, carrots and turnips)

5 l of water

100 g ginger

200 g garlic

100 g mustard seed ground

100 g dried red chilies ground

300 g salt

900 g mustard oil

200 g white vinegar

600 g jaggery

Chop and peel the carrots and turnips into roughly the same size pieces, about 3 cm cubes. Then remove the florets from the heads of cauliflower. You want everything to be roughly the same size, so it cooks and pickles evenly.

Bring 5 liters of water to a boil. Add the turnips to the pot of water, cover, and cook for two minutes. Remove from the turnips from water and repeat the same process with the carrots. Finally simply blanche the cauliflower in boiling water for 30 seconds. Thoroughly dry all vegetables and spread them out on kitchen towels to continue to air dry.

In a mortar and pestle combine and mash the ginger and garlic together until it forms a uniform paste. Add the ginger garlic paste and cold oil to a large pot. Turn on the stove and gently brown the ginger and garlic. Remove from heat. Add the mustard seeds and salt to the oil. Set aside for later.

Boil the jaggery in vinegar, stirring constantly until fully dissolved. Add this mixture to the oil. After the vinegar oil mixture has cooled to room temperature, add the vegetables and mix thoroughly with a dry wooden spoon. Place in sanitized pickle jars, cover and keep in the sun for about a week.

Wandering Fermented Chutney

10-15 habaneros (400-600 g)

5-10 dried red chilies5 

1 sweet capsicum

1 large carrot peeled

1tbsp mustard seeds

4-5 cloves of garlics

20 g fresh ginger

5-7 own black peppercorns

1 l of water

50 grams of salt

2 tbsp sorghum syrup 6

The Wandering Fermented Chutney is fundamentally a fermented hot sauce with the addition of mustard seeds and sorghum. Substituting the sweetness of the jaggery and with the rich molasses-tasting sorghum, a sweeter found in the American South. The number of and kind chilies in this recipe is adjustable based on the level of heat you want. I prefer to use the habaneros because of both their spiciness and fruitiness. If you prefer a mellower or sweeter hot sauce add more carrots, sweet peppers or even fresh citrus.

Start by roughly chopping all of the habaneros, capsicum, and carrot. Be sure to remove the pith from the capsicum. Roughly chop the ginger, peel and smash the garlic cloves. Add all of the chopped ingredients, the mustard seeds, dried chilies, 1 tbsp of sorghum and the peppercorns to a clean and sterilized fermentation safe jar. Try to add them in even layers, so everything is mixed together.

In a separate container mix the water and salt. Stir to combine and make sure the salt is completely dissolved. Add the water and salt mixture to the fermentation jar. Make sure the water covers everything completely, you may need to push down the vegetables with a wooden spoon or spatula. Do not tightly close the lid, either leave it slightly ajar or cover the top of the jar with cheese cloth. Now let it all ferment for 2-4 days. I recommend around 3 days but where you are making this, both the temperature and environment will have an effect on how long it takes to ferment. You should start to see signs of fermentation within the first 24 hours. For example tiny bubbles racing up the side of the jar and if you listen closely you will hear the snaps and pops inside the jar.

After 3-4 days empty the jar into a large bowl and strain the liquid. Keep all the liquid. With a hand blender or in a stand blender puree all the fermented chilies, spices, vegetables, along with another 1 tbsp of sorghum syrup. To thin out the puree add the strained liquid. I like the chutney to have a slightly thick texture, similar to a can of pureed tomatoes but add more liquid to get a thinner textured if desired. You will have extra liquid, do not throw it away. Use it as a marinade, a base to brine more vegetables or add it to a soup. It is full of flavor and should not be wasted.
